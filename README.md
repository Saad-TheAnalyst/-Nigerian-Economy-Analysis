# Nigerian Economy & Development Analysis (1960-2023)

## Overview
A comprehensive analysis of Nigeria's economic indicators 
from 1960 to 2023 using World Bank data, Python, Pandas, 
Matplotlib and Power BI. This project tells the story of 
Nigeria's economic journey — from oil boom to recession, 
through COVID-19 and into the post-subsidy removal era.

## Tools Used
- Python
- Pandas
- Matplotlib
- Power BI
- JupyterLab
- World Bank Open Data

## Questions Answered
- How has Nigeria's GDP grown over 60 years?
- What caused Nigeria's worst inflation periods?
- How much has the Naira depreciated against the Dollar?
- What was the economic impact of COVID-19 on Nigeria?
- How did the 2023 fuel subsidy removal affect Nigerians?
- How does Nigeria compare to Ghana, Kenya and South Africa?
- Is Nigeria getting richer or poorer per person?

## Key Findings

### GDP Growth
- Nigeria's highest GDP growth was 25.01% in 1970 
  during the oil boom era.
- The worst GDP contraction was -15.74% in 1967 
  during the Nigerian Civil War.
- COVID-19 caused a -6.37% GDP contraction in 2020 
  — the worst in over 30 years.
- Average GDP growth over 60 years was just 3.57% 
  showing inconsistent economic performance.

### Inflation & Currency
- Nigeria's worst inflation was 72.84% in 1995 
  during the Abacha military regime.
- The Naira depreciated by 534.43% against the Dollar 
  from 2000 to 2023 — from 101 Naira to 645 Naira 
  per Dollar.
- Fuel subsidy removal in 2023 pushed inflation from 
  18.85% in 2022 to 24.66% in 2023 and the exchange 
  rate jumped from 426 to 645 Naira per Dollar.

### Poverty & Living Standards
- Nigeria's poverty rate rose from 40.1% in 2018 
  to 56.2% in 2022 meaning over half of Nigerians 
  now live below the national poverty line.
- GDP per capita peaked at $3,189 in 2019 but 
  dropped to $2,138 in 2023 — Nigerians are 
  getting poorer per person despite population growth.
- Nigeria's population grew from 45 million in 1960 
  to 227 million in 2023 but GDP per capita has not 
  kept pace with this growth.

### Oil Dependency
- Oil rents peaked at 40.91% of GDP in 1979 
  showing Nigeria's extreme dependence on oil.
- Average oil rents were 12.34% of GDP over the 
  entire period confirming Nigeria as a petro-state.

### COVID-19 Impact
- GDP contracted by -6.37% in 2020
- Inflation rose from 11.40% in 2019 to 16.95% 
  in 2021 showing lasting economic damage.
- Recovery was slow with only 1.11% GDP growth 
  in 2021.

### 2023 Election Economic Impact
- GDP growth slowed from 4.32% in 2022 to 
  3.33% in 2023 after the election.
- Inflation worsened from 18.85% to 24.66%.
- The exchange rate jumped 51% from 426 to 
  645 Naira per Dollar in just one year.

### Nigeria vs African Peers
- South Africa consistently has the highest GDP 
  per capita among the four countries compared.
- Ghana has shown more stable inflation control 
  than Nigeria over the same period.
- Kenya has maintained more consistent GDP growth 
  with fewer extreme contractions than Nigeria.
- Nigeria has the largest population but not the 
  highest standard of living among its peers.

## The Real Story For Everyday Nigerians
Between 2000 and 2023 the Naira lost 534% of its 
value against the Dollar. A Nigerian earning 100,000 
Naira in 2000 would need 634,000 Naira today to 
maintain the same purchasing power. Combined with 
a poverty rate of 56.2% and inflation of 24.66% 
in 2023, the data tells a clear story — Nigeria's 
economic growth has not translated into better lives 
for the majority of its citizens.
